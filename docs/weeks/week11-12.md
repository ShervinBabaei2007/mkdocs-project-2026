# High-Fidelity Wireframes

> **Summary:** This week I moved from structure to visual design, producing high-fidelity wireframes for the PNWX redesign. The visual decisions - typography, color, and layout - were each tied to a specific usability goal rather than personal preference. Three Nielsen Norman heuristics guided every major choice.

---

## Where This Fits in the Project

If you're jumping in here, here's the quick context: I'm redesigning [Pacific Northwest X-Ray Inc.](https://www.pnwx.com/) - a medical imaging supplier with a cluttered, hard-to-navigate site. The [low-fidelity wireframes (Week 7/8)](week7-and8.md) defined the structure. This week, I gave that structure a visual language.

---

## The Core Problem

The original site suffers from visual noise - competing colors, inconsistent styling, and skull icons on navigation links. Every element fights for attention, so nothing stands out.

My redesign strips this back to essentials and makes every visual choice functional.

---

## Visual Decisions & Their Rationale

### Typography

I paired **Raleway** (headers) with **Inter** (body text). Raleway gives the brand a distinct character; Inter is optimized for screen legibility at small sizes. Together they balance identity with readability.

### Color Palette

I moved to a three-color system with a clear job for each:

- **Deep Navy** - establishes stability and structure
- **Light Blue** - supports secondary layout elements without competing
- **Green** - reserved exclusively for buttons and calls to action

Users always know where to click because green appears nowhere else.

---

## Three Heuristics Behind the Design

### Heuristic #1 - Aesthetic and Minimalist Design

Product cards show only what users need to make a decision. No decorative clutter competing with the product itself.

### Heuristic #4 - Consistency and Standards

Navigation looks and behaves the same on every page. Users build a mental model once and it holds throughout the site.

### Heuristic #6 - Recognition Over Recall

On-screen filters and sidebar categories let users find products by scanning - not by memorizing a site map.

## What I Learned

Anchoring visual decisions to heuristics kept me from making choices based on personal taste. Every time I picked a color or font, I had to ask: "What usability problem does this solve?" That constraint made the design more defensible - and better.
