# Glossary

This page explains technical terms and design concepts used throughout this site. If you come across an unfamiliar word in any weekly reflection, you can find a plain-language definition here.

---

**Crazy 8s**
A fast-paced ideation exercise where you sketch eight different ideas in eight minutes. The goal is quantity over quality - forcing you to move past your first instinct and explore a wider range of solutions before committing to one.

---

**Design Thinking**
A problem-solving approach that starts with understanding people before proposing solutions. It follows five stages: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test. The key idea is to define the problem clearly before jumping to answers.

---

**Figma**
A browser-based design tool used to create wireframes, prototypes, and user interface layouts. It's widely used in UX and product design because it's easy to iterate on and share with collaborators.

---

**Five Ws**
A framework for defining a problem by answering five questions: Who is affected? What is the issue? Where does it occur? When does it happen? Why does it matter? In UX, it helps designers focus on user needs before jumping to solutions.

---

**High-Fidelity Wireframe**
A detailed, near-final mockup of a design that includes real typography, colors, and content. It closely resembles the finished product and is used to test visual decisions and refine the user experience before development.

---

**Heuristic**
A usability guideline used to evaluate whether a design is easy to use. The Nielsen Norman Group's 10 heuristics are the most widely used set - they cover things like consistency, error prevention, and minimalist design.

---

**Information Architecture (IA)**
The practice of organizing and labeling content so users can find what they need. It covers navigation structure, page hierarchy, and the language used to describe categories - essentially, how a site is structured from the user's point of view.

---

**Low-Fidelity Wireframe**
A rough, simplified layout of a design - often in black and white with placeholder content. It focuses on structure and navigation rather than visuals, making it easy to test ideas quickly and cheaply before investing in detailed design work.

---

**Persona**
A fictional but research-based character that represents a type of user. Personas include goals, frustrations, and behaviors to help designers make decisions that serve real people rather than abstract assumptions.

---

**UX (User Experience)**
How a person feels when using a product or website - covering ease of use, clarity, efficiency, and overall satisfaction. Good UX means users can complete their goals without frustration or confusion.

---

**User Flow**
A diagram showing the steps a user takes to complete a task on a website or app. It maps out every screen or decision point from start to finish, helping designers spot gaps or unnecessary friction in the experience.

---

**User Scenario**
A short narrative describing how a specific user might interact with a product in a real-world context. Scenarios help designers think through edge cases and ensure the design works for realistic situations, not just ideal ones.

---

**User Story**
A short, goal-focused statement written from a user's perspective, usually in the format: *"As a [type of user], I want to [do something] so that [I achieve a goal]."* User stories keep design decisions tied to real needs rather than features for their own sake.
